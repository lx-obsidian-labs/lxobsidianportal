Working at Heights

Working at Heights (Fall Arrest)

SAQA 229998NQF Level 12 CreditsDuration: 2 Days
1

Introduction to Heights Safety

This module introduces the fundamental concepts of working at heights in the mining industry. Understanding fall hazards is critical for every mine worker as falls from height remain one of the leading causes of fatalities in mining operations worldwide. This comprehensive training covers the legal requirements, hazard identification, and preventive measures essential for safe work at elevation.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
1.1 Understanding Fall Hazards in Mining
  • Falls from elevated work platforms, scaffolds, and ladders
  • Falls into openings in floors, roofs, and walls
  • Falls from vehicles and mobile equipment
  • Falls during underground mining operations (shafts, stopes)
  • Slips and trips on elevated working surfaces
  • Statistics: Falls account for approximately 25-30% of all mining fatalities globally
1.2 Legislative Requirements
  • Mine Health and Safety Act (MHSA) Section 11: Employer duties to provide safe working environment
  • Occupational Health and Safety Act (OHSA) 85 of 1993: General workplace safety requirements
  • Regulation 13 of the MHSA: Specific requirements for working at height
  • SANS 10085: Code of practice for fall protection
  • Client responsibilities under construction regulations
1.3 Roles and Responsibilities
  • Employer: Provide safe systems of work, equipment, training, and supervision
  • Supervisor: Ensure procedures are followed, conduct risk assessments
  • Worker: Use equipment correctly, report hazards, follow procedures
  • Fall Protection Planner: Develop and implement fall protection plans
  • Competent Person: Inspect equipment and systems

Key Point: Safety Representatives have legal powers under Section 18 of the OHSA to inspect workplaces and report hazards.

1.4 Hazard Identification
  • Unprotected edges and openings
  • Improperly constructed scaffolding
  • Missing or inadequate guardrails
  • Unsafe access and egress
  • Poor housekeeping on elevated surfaces
  • Weather conditions affecting stability
  • Human factors: fatigue, complacency, rushing

  2. Explain in detail the legal requirements under MHSA Section 11 and Regulation 13
  3. Describe the roles and responsibilities of each person involved in height work
  4. Create a comprehensive checklist for identifying hazards before starting height work
  5. Research and document at least 3 case studies of fall-related incidents in mining

Fall Arrest Equipment

This module covers the selection, inspection, and proper use of fall arrest equipment. Understanding your equipment is essential for your safety when working at heights. We will examine various types of equipment, their applications, and the critical importance of regular inspection.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
2.1 Full Body Harnesses
  • Types: Standard full body, vest-style, work positioning, rescue
  • Components: Shoulder straps, leg straps, chest strap, dorsal D-ring, side D-rings, waist belt
  • Selection Criteria: Size, fit, intended use, environmental conditions
  • Standards: SANS 50361, EN 361, ANSI Z359.11
  • Weight Capacity: Ensure rated for user weight including tools and equipment
2.2 Lanyards and Energy Absorbers
  • Types: Fixed length, adjustable, retractable (SRL)
  • Energy Absorbers (YA Type): Limit impact force to maximum 6kN
  • Length: Standard 1.8m (6ft) - must account for in fall clearance calculation
  • Materials: Webbing, wire rope, synthetic rope
  • Connection: Snap hooks with 22kN minimum breaking strength
2.3 Anchor Points and Connectors
  • Anchor Points: Must support minimum 22kN or designed by engineer
  • Types: Fixed D-rings, beam clamps, concrete anchors, engineered anchors
  • Karabiners: 22kN minimum, triple-action preferred for mining
  • D-Rings: Should be forged steel, not welded
  • Anchor Selection: Must be capable of withstanding fall forces
2.4 Inspection Procedures
  • Pre-Use Inspection: Visual check before each use
  • Detailed Inspection: Monthly or as per manufacturer guidelines
  • Inspection Points:
    • Harness: Straps, stitching, buckles, D-rings, labels
    • Lanyard: Webbing cuts/frays, stitching, hooks, energy absorber pack
    • Connectors: Gate mechanism, body, locking device

Critical: All fall arrest equipment must be inspected before EACH use. Any equipment showing signs of damage MUST be removed from service immediately.

2.5 Maintenance and Storage
  • Clean equipment with mild soap and water
  • Store in cool, dry, clean environment away from direct sunlight
  • Never use bleach, solvents, or petroleum-based cleaners
  • Allow to dry completely before storage
  • Hang harnesses to maintain shape
  • Document all inspections and maintenance
2.6 Discard Criteria
  • Any strap cut, broken, or abraded more than 1/3 width
  • Burned, melted, or chemically damaged webbing
  • Bent, broken, or damaged hardware
  • Missing or illegible labels
  • Evidence of shock load or fall arrest
  • Exceeding manufacturer expiry date

Fall Arrest Techniques

Learn the proper techniques for using fall arrest equipment, including correct harness fitting, connecting to anchor points, and understanding the difference between work positioning and fall arrest systems.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
3.1 Proper Harness Fitting
  • Adjust shoulder straps to prevent slippage
  • Leg straps should be snug but allow movement
  • Chest strap positioned across upper chest
  • Waist belt positioned above hip bones
  • Dorsal D-ring centered between shoulder blades
  • All adjustments should prevent sliding or rotation
3.2 Connection Techniques
  • Always connect to anchor point above D-ring level when possible
  • Use shortest practical lanyard length
  • Ensure lanyard doesn't cross over sharp edges
  • Connect snap hook to D-ring, never to harness loops
  • Verify gate is fully closed and locked
  • Never connect two snap hooks together
3.3 Work Positioning vs Fall Arrest
  • Work Positioning: System allows worker to work hands-free, limits fall to 600mm
  • Fall Arrest: System stops fall after it begins, requires adequate clearance
  • Positioning systems should have secondary fall arrest backup
  • Understand when each system is appropriate
3.4 Fall Clearance Calculations

Formula: Required clearance = Lanyard Length (1.8m) + Deceleration Distance (1.75m) + Worker Height (1.8m) + Safety Margin (1m) = Minimum 6.35m

  • Always calculate required fall clearance before working
  • Account for lanyard length, energy absorber extension, and user height
  • Consider swing fall hazards when anchor is not directly overhead
  • Add safety margin of at least 1 meter
3.5 Suspension Trauma Prevention
  • Blood pooling in legs can cause suspension trauma
  • Train worker to move legs if suspended
  • Use suspension trauma relief straps
  • Rescue must occur within 15-30 minutes
  • Medical attention required after any suspension

Danger: Never exceed the maximum free fall distance of 6 feet (1.8m). Always ensure adequate fall clearance below your work area before starting any height work.

  3. Calculate fall clearance for a given scenario (minimum 3 examples)

  5. Describe emergency rescue procedures for suspended worker

Scaffolding Fundamentals

This module covers the basic knowledge required for scaffolding work, including types of scaffolding, components, load classifications, and site preparation requirements.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
1.1 Types of Scaffolding
  • Tube and Fitting Scaffolding: Traditional system using steel tubes and clamps
  • System Scaffolding: Modular system with pre-engineered components (Layher, Cuplock, Ringlock)
  • Mobile Scaffolding: Wheels for mobility, used for maintenance work
  • Cantilever Scaffolding: Supported from structure, used when ground access unavailable
  • Suspended Scaffolding: Hung from overhead structure for high-rise work
  • Heavy Duty Scaffolding: Designed for heavy loads, industrial applications
1.2 Scaffolding Components
  • Standards: Vertical tubes, main load-bearing members
  • Ledgers: Horizontal tubes, support for transoms
  • Transoms: Horizontal tubes across ledgers, support platform
  • Planks: Walking/working surface (steel or timber)
  • Base Plates: Distribute load to foundation
  • Sole Boards: Distribute load on soft ground
  • Bracing: Provide stability (X-bracing, plan bracing)
  • Guardrails: Fall protection at platform edges
  • Toeboards: Prevent materials falling from platform
  • Ladder Access: Safe means of access to platform
1.3 Load Classifications (SANS 10085)
  • Light Duty: 25 kg/m² - Inspection, painting, light maintenance
  • Medium Duty: 50 kg/m² - General construction, brickwork
  • Heavy Duty: 75 kg/m² - Heavy materials, structural work
  • Special Duty: Engineer designed - Specific heavy loads

Note: According to SANS 10085, scaffolds exceeding 5m in height require special consideration and may require engineering design.

1.4 Site Preparation
  • Ground conditions must be assessed and capable of supporting loads
  • Underground services must be identified and protected
  • Adjacent structures must be assessed for tie-in points
  • Access and egress routes must be established
  • Materials storage and handling areas identified
  • Environmental factors considered (wind, weather)

Erection Procedures

Step-by-step procedures for safely erecting scaffolding, including base setup, standard erection, ledger and transom placement, bracing, and platform construction.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
2.1 Base Setup
  • Clear and level ground
  • Position sole boards on firm ground
  • Install base plates on sole boards
  • Ensure base plates are centered
  • Maximum bay length: 2.4m
  • Maximum bay width: 1.2m
2.2 Standard Erection
  • Install first lift standards (base to working platform)
  • Standards spaced according to design
  • Plumb and brace standards
  • Install ledgers at platform levels
  • Install transoms across ledgers
  • Continue lift by lift to required height
2.3 Platform Construction
  • Platforms minimum 600mm wide
  • Full planking (no gaps exceeding 40mm)
  • Platforms fully decked (minimum)
  • Double planks at edges for toeboards
  • Platforms secured to prevent movement
2.4 Guardrails and Toeboards
  • Top guardrail at 950mm minimum height
  • Mid rail at 500mm minimum height
  • Toeboards minimum 150mm height
  • All components secured in place
2.5 Bracing and Ties
  • Plan bracing at 4th bay intervals
  • Transverse bracing at lifts
  • Scaffold ties to structure every 4m height
  • Ties must resist tension and compression

3

Dismantling Procedures

Safe procedures for dismantling scaffolding in the correct sequence to prevent accidents and equipment damage.

3.1 Planning
  • Review original scaffold design
  • Plan dismantling sequence
  • Identify hazards and controls
  • Ensure adequate manpower and equipment
  • Establish material handling and storage areas
3.2 Safe Dismantling Sequence
  • Dismantle in reverse order of erection
  • Remove guardrails and toeboards first
  • Remove platform planks from top down
  • Remove transoms and ledgers
  • Lower components safely (do not throw)
  • Remove bracing and ties
  • Dismantle standards last

Danger: Never throw scaffold components from height. All materials must be lowered using ropes or chute systems.

3.3 Material Handling
  • Use rope lowering systems
  • chute systems for small components
  • Stack components neatly for inspection
  • Sort damaged vs serviceable equipment
  • Clean and maintain before storage
3.4 Documentation
  • Complete scaffold dismantling register
  • Record any damaged components
  • Report any issues to supervisor
  • Sign off completed work

Basic Rigging Operations

SAQA 253582NQF Level 28 CreditsDuration: 5 Days
1

Rigging Fundamentals

Core knowledge required for rigging operations including roles, load calculations, centre of gravity, and communication signals.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
1.1 Roles and Responsibilities
  • Rigger: Selects and inspects rigging equipment, attaches loads
  • Signal Person: Communicates with crane/equipment operator
  • Lift Supervisor: Overall responsibility for lift planning and execution
  • Crane Operator: Operates lifting equipment, follows signals
  • Competent Rigger: Has training, experience, authority to make decisions
1.2 Load Weight Estimation
  • Calculate volume × density of material
  • Add weight of any containers, packaging, or attachments
  • Always round up to ensure adequate capacity
  • Use manufacturer's data where available
  • When in doubt, treat as heavier

Formula: Weight = Volume × Density. Example: Steel plate 2m × 1m × 0.02m = 0.04m³ × 7850 kg/m³ = 314 kg

1.3 Centre of Gravity
  • Point where weight is evenly distributed
  • Lift hook must be directly over centre of gravity
  • Determine COG from design drawings or calculation
  • Adjust sling positions to align with COG
  • Off-centre loads cause swinging and instability
1.4 Hand Signals
  • Stop: Arm extended upward, palm forward
  • Raise/Lower: Arm extended, thumb up/down
  • Travel: Arm extended in direction of travel
  • Emergency Stop: Both arms extended upward, fists clenched
  • Dog Everything: Arms crossed over chest
1.5 Safe Working Loads

Formula: Working Load Limit (WLL) = Safe Working Load (SWL) × Design Factor. Always use WLL for calculations.

  • SWL (Safe Working Load) - older term
  • WLL (Working Load Limit) - current standard
  • Design Factor typically 5:1 for wire rope
  • Always verify WLL from equipment identification

Manual Lifting Equipment

Understanding and proper use of manual lifting equipment including wire rope slings, chain slings, webbing slings, and shackles.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
2.1 Wire Rope Slings
  • Configurations:
    • Straight (vertical lift)
    • Choker (grip load around circumference)
    • Basket (supports load in U-shape)
  • Eye Types: Flemish eye, thimble eye
  • Capacity: Varies with diameter, construction, and configuration
  • Advantage: High strength, heat resistant, durable
2.2 Chain Slings
  • Grades: 80 (highest), 100, 120
  • Components: Master link, chain, coupling links
  • Configurations: Single leg, 2-leg, 3-leg, 4-leg
  • Advantage: Adjustable, can be used in high temperatures
  • Inspection: More thorough inspection required
2.3 Webbing Slings
  • Types: Polyester, nylon, polypropylene
  • Configurations: Eye & eye, endless, triangle
  • Advantages: Lightweight, flexible, won't scratch load
  • Limitations: Not for high temperatures, susceptible to damage
2.4 Shackles
  • Types: Bow (anchor), Pin (chain)
  • Pin Styles: Screw pin, safety pin, round pin
  • Capacity: Clearly marked on body
  • Always: Seat pin fully, use safety pins where required
2.5 Inspection and Discard Criteria

Important: All slings must be visually inspected before each use. Remove any sling from service if it shows signs of damage, excessive wear, or has been subjected to shock loading.

  • Wire Rope: Broken wires, kinking, flattening, corrosion
  • Chain: Stretched links, cracked weld, bent links, excessive wear
  • Webbing: Cuts, burns, holes, excessive wear, UV damage
  • Shackles: Bent, cracked, damaged pin or body

1

Introduction to OHSA

The Occupational Health and Safety Act (OHSA) 85 of 1993 is the primary legislation governing workplace safety in South Africa.

1.1 Purpose and Scope
  • Provide for health and safety of persons at work
  • Protect persons against hazards
  • Establish health and safety structures
  • Promote occupational health
1.2 Duties of Employers
  • Provide safe plant and equipment
  • Provide safe systems of work
  • Ensure safe use, handling, storage of substances
  • Provide information, training, and supervision
  • Maintain healthy work environment
  • Report accidents and diseases
1.3 Duties of Employees
  • Take care of their own health and safety
  • Cooperate with employer
  • Follow safety procedures
  • Report hazards and incidents
  • Use provided PPE correctly
1.4 Role of Health and Safety Representative
  • Represent employees on health and safety matters
  • Conduct workplace inspections
  • Investigate incidents
  • Attend health and safety committee meetings
  • Report hazards to employer

Key Point: Safety Representatives have legal powers under Section 18 of the OHSA to inspect workplaces and report hazards.

2

Hazard Identification and Risk Assessment

2.1 Types of Workplace Hazards
  • Physical: Noise, vibration, radiation, temperature extremes, moving machinery
  • Chemical: Dust, fumes, gases, liquids, solids
  • Biological: Bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
  • Ergonomic: Repetitive motion, poor posture, manual handling
  • Psychosocial: Stress, bullying, fatigue, shift work
2.2 Risk Assessment Methodology
  • Severity: How serious could the outcome be? (1-5)
  • Probability: How likely is it to happen? (1-5)
  • Exposure: How often is person exposed? (1-5)
  • Risk Rating: Severity × Probability × Exposure (Max 125)

Risk Matrix: Low (1-25): Monitor | Medium (26-50): Control within specified time | High (51-75): Urgent action | Critical (76-125): Stop work immediately

2.3 Hierarchy of Controls
  • 1. Elimination: Remove hazard completely
  • 2. Substitution: Replace with less hazardous
  • 3. Engineering: Isolate people from hazard
  • 4. Administrative: Change work practices
  • 5. PPE: Protect worker with personal equipment

Machine Knowledge

Comprehensive knowledge of dump truck types, components, controls, and systems.

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
1.1 Types of Dump Trucks
  • Rigid Body Dump Trucks:
    • capacities from 30 to 400 tons
    • Used for large-scale mining operations
    • Articulated at chassis frame
    • Examples: CAT 785, Komatsu 930E, Liebherr T 284
  • Articulated Dump Trucks:
    • capacities from 25 to 40 tons
    • Greater mobility on rough terrain
    • Two-piece articulated frame
    • Examples: CAT 725, Volvo A40, Komatsu HM400
  • Electric Drive Trucks:
    • Direct drive from diesel to generator
    • AC electric drive motors in wheels
    • Most efficient for haul distances over 5km
1.2 Major Components
  • Chassis: Main frame supporting all components
  • Body: Load carrying container
  • Engine: Typically diesel, 350-3500 HP
  • Transmission: Automatic shifting, multiple speeds
  • Hydraulic System: Body raise/lower, steering
  • Electrical System: Starting, lighting, instruments
  • Brake System: Service, park, emergency brakes
  • Suspension: Mechanical or hydraulic
1.3 Cab Controls and Instruments
  • Steering wheel and column
  • Accelerator and brake pedals
  • Transmission selector (P-R-N-D)
  • Body raise/lower lever
  • Gauges: Speedometer, tachometer, fuel, temperature, pressure
  • Warning lights and indicators
  • ROPS/FOPS certified cab

Tip: Familiarize yourself with all gauges and warning lights before operating. Know the location of emergency exits and fire extinguisher.

2

Pre-Operation Procedures

2.1 Walk-Around Inspection Checklist
  • Before Starting:
    • Check for leaks (oil, hydraulic fluid, coolant)
    • Inspect tires (pressure, damage, objects embedded)
    • Check body and chassis for damage
    • Verify all guards are in place
    • Check lights and reflectors
    • Inspect mirrors and windows
2.2 Engine Start-Up
  • Ensure transmission in Park
  • Check seat belt is fastened
  • Turn key to start position
  • Allow engine to warm up
  • Check all gauges and warning lights
  • Test brakes before moving

Critical: Never operate a dump truck with defective brakes, steering, or suspension. Report all defects immediately.

Operating Procedures

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
3.1 Loading Procedures
  • Position truck correctly at loading point
  • Ensure loader operator sees you
  • Stay clear of loading equipment swing radius
  • Wait for load to settle before moving
  • Do not exceed rated payload
  • Spillage on body must be cleaned before hauling
3.2 Hauling Procedures
  • Check route and traffic before entering
  • Maintain safe following distance
  • Observe speed limits
  • Use horn at intersections and blind spots
  • Lower body before descending grades
  • Watch for pedestrians and other vehicles
3.3 Dumping Procedures
  • Select firm, level ground for dumping
  • Ensure adequate clearance for body raise
  • Check overhead clearances (power lines, structures)
  • Engage park brake before raising body
  • Raise body slowly, watch for instability
  • Lower body completely before moving
3.4 Grade and Slope Operation
  • Maximum grade usually 10-15% depending on conditions
  • Fully loaded trucks should descend empty
  • Use engine retarder on long descents
  • Maintain momentum but control speed
  • Never shift to neutral on downgrade

First Aid

Basic First Aid

SAQA 119567NQF Level 15 Credits
1

Principles of First Aid

Required Video
Watch Video on YouTube
1.1 Primary Survey - DRS ABC
  • D - Danger: Check for danger to you and casualty
  • R - Response: Check if casualty is responsive
  • S - Send for Help: Call emergency services
  • A - Airway: Open and clear airway
  • B - Breathing: Check for normal breathing
  • C - CPR: Start CPR if not breathing normally
1.2 Calling Emergency Services
  • Know local emergency numbers (10111 for South Africa)
  • Give precise location
  • Describe nature of emergency
  • Number of casualties
  • Age and condition of casualties
  • First aid being given
  • Stay on line until told to hang up

Key Point: For every minute without CPR, the chance of survival decreases by 10%. Early CPR can double or triple survival rates.

2

CPR Techniques

2.1 Adult CPR (18 years and over)
  • Place casualty on firm surface
  • Position hands on centre of chest (lower half of sternum)
  • Compress chest 5-6cm deep
  • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute
  • Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 breaths
  • Continue until help arrives or casualty responds
2.2 Child CPR (1-18 years)
  • Use one or two hands (depending on size)
  • Compress about 5cm (1/3 depth of chest)
  • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute
  • Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 breaths
2.3 Infant CPR (Under 1 year)
  • Use two fingers on breastbone just below nipple line
  • Compress about 4cm (1/3 depth of chest)
  • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute
  • Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 breaths
  • Do not give rescue breaths by mouth-to-mouth - use mouth and nose

Wounds and Bleeding

3.1 Types of Wounds
  • Incision: Clean cut, sharp object (knife, glass)
  • Laceration: Jagged edges, tearing
  • Puncture: Small entry wound (nail, needle)
  • Abrasion: Surface scrape (road rash)
  • Avulsion: Tissue torn away
  • Amputation: Body part completely severed
3.2 Controlling Bleeding
  • Direct Pressure: Apply firm pressure with clean cloth
  • Elevation: Raise injured area above heart
  • Pressure Points: Apply pressure to major arteries
  • Tourniquet: Last resort for life-threatening bleeding

Warning: Apply tourniquet only as last resort for life-threatening limb bleeding. Note time of application.
